Estate Flow

How the Estate Flows

This diagram traces the path of assets from either spouse's death through the trust structure. The same flow applies to both Mark's and Claire's plans. IRA assets flow separately through the standalone IRA Trusts (see IRA Trusts tab).

Estate Distribution Flowchart — Either Spouse's Death
Distribution Scenarios

How Assets Distribute in Each Scenario

A

First Spouse Dies — Surviving Spouse Receives Everything

All trust assets pass outright to the surviving spouse with no restrictions. The surviving spouse receives the assets free and clear — not in trust — and can use, invest, or give them away as they choose.

The surviving spouse has the option to disclaim (renounce) some or all of the inheritance. Any disclaimed assets flow into the Family Trust instead, where they are managed for the benefit of the spouse and descendants under trustee discretion. This is a tax planning tool — disclaiming into the Family Trust can shelter assets from the surviving spouse's taxable estate.

Critical deadline: A qualified disclaimer under IRC §2518 must be executed in writing within 9 months of the deceased spouse's date of death. The surviving spouse must not accept the property or receive any benefits from it prior to disclaimer. Per the attorney's memo, the Murrays should contact Duncan Spratt upon either spouse's death to ensure the ability to disclaim is preserved and properly completed.

B

Both Spouses Deceased (or No Surviving Spouse) — Family Trust

If there is no surviving spouse (either because the second spouse has now died, or both died simultaneously), all assets flow into the Family Trust (Article V). During the Family Trust phase, the surviving spouse (if still alive) is the primary beneficiary, with descendants also eligible for discretionary distributions.

The Family Trust continues until the Division Date — the later of: (i) the surviving spouse's death, and (ii) the youngest living child reaching age 25. At that point, the trust splits into two components: the Residence Trust (for real property) and individual Descendant's Trusts (for everything else).

C

Division Date Reached — Residence Trust + Descendant's Trusts

Residence Trust: All trust-owned real property is carved out and held in a separate Residence Trust. Eli has the right to reside rent-free for his lifetime in any property held here. The other children (Landon and Blakely) may also use the property rent-free but cannot displace Eli. The trustee may sell real property that Eli is not using as his primary residence, with proceeds flowing to the Descendant's Trusts. Upon Eli's death, all remaining Residence Trust property is distributed to the Descendant's Trusts.

Descendant's Trusts (Article VI): All non-real-property assets are divided among the grantor's then-living descendants, per stirpes, with each share held in a separate trust. Each Descendant's Trust must contribute its proportional share to the Residence Trust expenses (taxes, insurance, maintenance) before making other distributions. The primary beneficiary receives discretionary distributions for support and comfort, with remaining assets available for that beneficiary's own descendants.

D

No Surviving Descendants — Default Distribution

If no lineal descendant of the grantor survives, the default provision (Article VII) distributes assets one-half to the grantor's then-living heirs and one-half to the grantor's spouse's then-living heirs, determined under Georgia intestacy law.

Open Item — Article VII (Remote Takers): Per the attorney's memo (Nov. 21, 2025), the default distribution provision in the new Revocable Trusts differs from the provisions in the Murrays' prior Wills. Duncan Spratt has asked the Murrays whether Article VII should be updated to mirror their prior Wills or to include trusts for specific family members. This item needs to be resolved before signing.

Mechanics

How the IRA Trusts Work

Dormant Until Funded
$10 Initial Gift

Each trust is funded with only $10 at creation. The trusts are effectively dormant until retirement plan assets are added — either during Mark's lifetime or at his death via beneficiary designation. If no assets beyond the $10 are ever added, the trust automatically terminates at Mark's death and the $10 is paid to the Trustee as a termination fee (Item II(c)).

Irrevocability Triggers
Three Triggering Events

Each trust becomes irrevocable upon the first of: (i) Mark's primary care physician determines he is under legal disability, (ii) Mark's death, or (iii) Mark's written amendment waiving his right to revoke. Until then, Mark retains full power to amend or revoke (Item I).

RMD Compliance
Required Minimum Distributions

The Trustee must receive all required minimum distributions from the retirement plan, but is not required to pass those distributions through to the beneficiary (Item IV(a)). The Trustee receives the RMD into the trust and then exercises discretion on whether and how much to distribute to the beneficiary. The Trustee may also accelerate distributions beyond the RMD if in the beneficiary's best interest (Item XII).

Distribution Priority
Welfare Over Preservation

Mark's express intent across all three trusts is that each child's welfare be provided for as well as possible, rather than preserving principal for future generations. The Trustee is directed to be guided by this consideration when determining distribution amounts (Item IV(a)).

Designated Beneficiary Compliance: All three trusts include specific provisions (Item IV(b), IV(c)(2)) ensuring that any exercise of the power of appointment involving retirement plan assets is limited to trusts that qualify as "designated beneficiaries" under IRC §401(a)(9). This preserves the ability to stretch distributions over the beneficiary's life expectancy where permitted by law.

Succession

After the Primary Beneficiary's Death

Power of Appointment
Limited Power — During Life or at Death

Each child holds a limited power of appointment exercisable by written instrument during life or by will at death. They may direct trust assets to any descendant of Mark who is assigned to a younger generation for GSTT purposes. They may not appoint to themselves, their estate, their creditors, or the creditors of their estate.

Default Distribution
Per Stirpes to Descendants

If the power of appointment is not exercised, remaining trust assets divide per stirpes into separate trusts for the deceased child's living descendants. If the child has no living descendants, assets divide per stirpes among Mark's other living descendants. If Mark has no surviving descendants, assets split 50/50 between Mark's heirs and Claire's heirs under Georgia intestacy law.

Successor Trust Distributions
Mandatory Age-Based Payouts for Next-Generation Trusts

When trust assets pass to the next generation (trusts created under Item IV(c) for descendants), those successor trusts include a mandatory distribution schedule that does not apply to Eli, Landon, or Blakely's own trusts:

  • Age 35: Trustee distributes one-third (1/3) of the trust to the beneficiary
  • Age 45: Trustee distributes one-half (1/2) of remaining trust assets
  • Age 65: Trustee distributes all remaining trust assets outright

This schedule applies only to trusts created for the grandchildren's generation and beyond — not to Eli, Landon, or Blakely's primary trusts, which have no forced distributions at any age.

Beneficiary Self-Trusteeship (Revocable Trust + IRA Trusts)
Landon & Blakely — Trustee Graduation Rights

Landon and Blakely (but not Eli) each have the option to step into the trustee role over their own trusts — both their Descendant's Trust under the Revocable Trust and their standalone IRA Trust:

  • Age 35: May elect to become sole trustee, replacing the existing trustee

While serving as their own trustee, distributions to themselves are limited to the support and education standard (accustomed manner of living). They may appoint a co-trustee who can exercise broader discretionary distribution powers on their behalf.

Limitations

Trustee Power Limitations

When a beneficiary also serves as trustee, their powers are deliberately restricted to avoid adverse estate tax consequences. These limitations apply consistently across both Revocable Trusts and all three IRA Trusts.

Surviving Spouse as Trustee
HEMS Standard Applies

While serving as trustee of the Family Trust (Article V), the surviving spouse may only make discretionary distributions to the extent necessary for a beneficiary's health, maintenance, education, or support in reasonable comfort. A co-trustee may exercise broader powers. The spouse also cannot distribute to satisfy personal support obligations owed to another person.

Beneficiary as Trustee
Self-Distribution Restrictions

Any individual trustee who is also a beneficiary is limited to support and education distributions to themselves, cannot exercise incidents of ownership over insurance on their own life held in the trust, and cannot distribute to discharge personal support obligations. These restrictions prevent trust assets from being included in the trustee-beneficiary's taxable estate.

Workaround Built In: The trusts are designed so that a beneficiary serving as trustee can appoint a co-trustee who is not subject to these limitations. The co-trustee can then exercise full discretionary distribution power on the beneficiary's behalf — providing access to trust assets while preserving the estate tax protection.

Features

Key Trust Features & Protections

Dynasty Structure
360-Year Trust Term

Georgia permits trusts lasting up to 360 years from the grantor's death. All trusts under this plan — both Revocable Trusts and all three IRA Trusts — use that maximum term, allowing multi-generational wealth preservation.

Creditor Protection
Spendthrift Clause

Beneficiary interests cannot be transferred, assigned, or reached by creditors. If a creditor blocks distribution, the trustee holds and accumulates until direct distribution becomes possible.

Age Protection
Holdback Trust Under Age 30

The trustee may withhold any distribution to an individual under age 30, holding assets in trust with discretionary distributions until the recipient reaches that age.

Survivorship
90-Day Survival Requirement

Under the Revocable Trusts, a beneficiary must survive the grantor (or other triggering event) by at least 90 days to inherit. This prevents assets from passing through a beneficiary's estate if both spouses die in close succession.

Tax Optimization
Portability Election

Both Wills direct the executor to elect portability — transferring the deceased spouse's unused federal estate tax exemption to the surviving spouse's estate, maximizing the combined exemption amount.

Flexibility
Special Power of Appointment

Each primary beneficiary can redirect trust assets at death to the grantor's descendants, qualified charities, or a Spousal Trust — providing flexibility for future circumstances.

Investment
Diversification Waiver

The grantor expressly waives the trustee's duty to diversify concentrated positions, permitting the trustee to retain concentrated holdings without liability for failing to sell.

Disclosure
Information Waiver

The trustee has no duty to notify beneficiaries of the trust's existence or provide accountings. Disclosure is at the trustee's discretion. The IRA Trusts similarly exempt the Trustee from O.C.G.A. §53-12-242 (existence notification) and §53-12-243 (reporting).

Efficiency
Small Trust Termination

If any trust's fair market value falls below $100,000 (Article X(l)), the trustee may terminate it and distribute outright to the income beneficiaries in equal shares.

Incapacity

Incapacity & Lifetime Administration

During Lifetime
Full Grantor Control

While competent, each grantor retains complete control — directing all income and principal distributions, and retaining the power to revoke or amend the trust at any time for any reason. This applies to both the Revocable Trusts and the IRA Trusts.

Incapacity Trigger
Layered Determination Process

For the Revocable Trusts, incapacity is determined by a layered process (Article XIV(c)): first the grantor's spouse; then a majority of the grantor's adult children; then the grantor's primary care physician. No court proceeding is required.

For the IRA Trusts, incapacity is determined solely by the grantor's primary care physician (Item III(a)(i)). Upon incapacity, the grantor cannot revoke or amend and is deemed to have automatically resigned as Trustee.

Incapacity Management
Trustee Discretion for Spouse, Descendants & Gifting

During incapacity under the Revocable Trusts, the trustee may distribute income and principal for the benefit of the grantor, the grantor's spouse, and any descendants. The trustee may also continue any established gifting program on the grantor's behalf. If a court-appointed guardian exists, the guardian has no power over trust assets — only the trustee controls them.

Under the IRA Trusts, the successor trustee (Claire, then Cherie & Joshua) assumes management with the same discretionary distribution powers for the named child beneficiary and their descendants.

Tax Safeguards

Grantor Trust Restrictions (IRA Trusts)

All three IRA Trusts include identical provisions (Item VI) that prevent the trusts from being treated as grantor trusts for income tax purposes after they become irrevocable. These restrictions ensure that retirement plan distributions are taxed to the trust or its beneficiaries — not to Mark or Claire.

No Self-Dealing
Adequate Consideration Required

No trustee may deal with trust property for less than adequate consideration (Item VI(a)), and no contributor or their spouse may borrow from the trust without adequate interest and security (Item VI(b)).

No Insurance on Grantor
Life Insurance Prohibition

Trust assets may not be used to purchase or pay premiums on insurance on the life of any contributor or their spouse (Item VI(c)).

No Distributions to Grantor
Complete Wall Between Grantor & Trust

After irrevocability, no trust income or principal may be distributed to or for the benefit of Mark (or Claire as his spouse), nor may any distribution satisfy their personal support obligations (Item VI(d)). This creates a complete wall between the grantor and the trust assets, ensuring the trust is a separate taxpayer.

Georgia Law Limitations
What the Health Care Agent Cannot Do

Under Georgia law, a health care agent does not have the power to make decisions regarding:

  • Sterilization
  • Involuntary hospitalization or involuntary treatment for mental illness, developmental disability, or addictive disease
  • Any matter covered by a separate psychiatric advance directive under O.C.G.A. Chapter 11 of Title 37

Additionally, a future divorce or annulment automatically revokes the selection of a current spouse as health care agent. A future marriage revokes a non-spouse agent unless that person becomes the new spouse.

The agent is explicitly prohibited from exercising any incidents of ownership over insurance policies on the agent's own life, or exercising any power of appointment over such policies. This prevents the agent's estate tax inclusion of those policies.

Non-Family Agents
Self-Dealing Limitation

An agent who is not an ancestor, spouse, or descendant of the principal cannot use the principal's property to benefit themselves or anyone they owe an obligation of support, unless specifically authorized in the Special Instructions.

Conservator Nomination
Agent Nominated as Conservator

Both documents nominate the person serving as agent under the POA to also serve as court-appointed conservator of the principal's estate, if one becomes necessary. This aligns the court-supervised and voluntary authority frameworks.

Reliance
Third-Party Protection

Any person (including the agent) may rely on the validity of the POA or a copy unless they have actual knowledge it has been terminated or is invalid. This facilitates practical use with financial institutions and other third parties.
